Pretty vs Smart
In today's society, more often than not, a girl is expected to choose between being considered pretty or smart. It is from an early age that this kind of a message is instilled in our brains by the media.
This article explores this very principle in action. It discusses many people's favorite cartoon show, Scooby Doo and its portrayal of Velma as the smart, never sexualized girl, and Daphne, who is almost always sexualized but never considered intelligent.
We hope that this example really sheds light on the media's influence on girls starting very early in childhood.
The Punishing Dress Code
This post talks about a Labrador school (school in Labrador area) whose dress code unjustly targets girls. Keep in mind this is NOT an isolated incident.
Dress codes around the country have been coming under fire recently because they disproportionately punish female students. The onus is always on the girls to dress appropriately so that they don't distract male students, or male teachers.
The article further goes on to elaborate by saying that "Shaming girls for wearing bras or short skirts in hot weather plays very well into a culture that humiliates girls for how they look."
Like a girl
Stop and think to yourself. Have you ever been told that you do something (run,throw,hit etc) like a girl? Or maybe you haven't, but have you heard someone say that to your sister, or friend? You most likely have. Always campaign, #likeagirl is battling to change the way the phrase "like a girl is used" because
Using #LIKEAGIRL as an insult is a hard knock against any adolescent girl. And since the rest of puberty's really no picnic either, it's easy to see what a huge impact it can have on a girl's self-confidence. We[Always] have kicked off an epic battle to make sure that girls everywhere keep their confidence throughout puberty and beyond, and making a start by showing them that doing things #LIKEAGIRL is downright amazing.
The issue with "Boys will be boys"
The statement "boys will be boys" is problematic for several reasons. This article discusses why.

1. It prompts students to construct gender stereotypes.

2. Gender stereotypes allow unconscious biases to form and proliferate.

3. It is misinformed thinking and oversimplifies the problem.

4. It limits the full expression of children.

women are expected to constantly be attractive. We're expected to be subdued, quiet and well spoken. No one likes a woman who stands up and shouts about issues.
